Problem formulation, Method and Research Question
Human activities have had a significant impact on the earth’s ecosystems; especially in marine ecosystems. For centuries, ocean have been regarded as an inexhaustible source of food and a convenient dumping ground, too vast to be affected by anything we do. But in the last few decades, it has become increasingly clear that the ocean has limits and that in many important parts of our seas the sustainability thresholds have been breached. It is now, urgent to understand that the ocean has undergone large physical and biogeochemical modifications in response to human induced stressors. These stressors encompass ocean surface warming, changes in ocean salinity, modifications of density structure and stratification, as well as an increase in dissolved inorganic carbon concentrations and a decrease in seawater pH in response to ocean uptake of anthropogenic carbon. \citep{Doney_2010}
Current approaches to assess the risk of anthropogenic stressors to marine ecosystems are developed for single stressors. The cumulative impact of several stressors, however, may differ from the impact of the single stressors and can result in nonlinear effects and ecological surprises. The complexity of marine systems, their inherent high variability, and the influence of multiple environmental factors or stressors suggests that no single measure is adequate for assessing the effects of multiple stressors on biota.
Given this background, the objectives of this study are to develop and provide a literature review for assessing the effects of multiple stressors on marine systems and helping to understand the cumulative impact of several stressors. In the Anthropocene, where many stressors are interacting with each other and everything is connected in a complex web of effects, the methods we apply for impact assessments must necessarily reflect this reality, now, and especially in the times ahead if we are to understand the ongoing changes and where they’ll lead us.
Therefore, we tried to collect scientific articles, journals and books for the different categories of our research topic: the Anthropocene, ongoing changes and future development, ecosystem health, multiple stressor assessment methods and coral reefs. To make sure, that everyone gets the same knowledge, we divided the articles of each topics between the group members and took notes while reading. Through the writing progress, the topics were switched around, to check each other and give everyone the same knowledge. In between, the different sections and especially the results of our project were discussed in several group meetings.
So in the end we were able to answer our research question:
How do we assess the effects of multiple stressors impacting the marine ecosystem in the Anthropocene? 
This question is asking for assessment methods, metrics and/or scaling for the health status of a marine ecosystem affected by multiple stressors. Hereby, we focus on human-induced stressors, due to the fact that these stressors now-a-days are the biggest disturbances which challenge marine ecosystems. We want to tackle and solve this question by finding out firstly what actually defines a healthy ecosystem and how is ecosystem degradation generally defined. Furthermore, we need to know, what the main stressors affecting the marine ecosystem are, what the impacts of these harmful consequences are and especially how these do interact and might intersect.
By asking these questions, we will be able to get a more complex overview of which metrics we have to include in our assessment of ecosystem health and multiple stressors later on. With this foundation, we can move on to ask which methods are used or proposed to evaluate the impacts of multiple stressors in the marine environment. Moreover, we want to discuss how these methods compare and if these methods can be generalized to find an overall assessment method. These questions are elaborating the main particular question for assessment methods and questioning their effectivity and especially their integrity and perspicuity. Moving on from that, the question that comes up is: at which levels can the found methods be applied?
Thus, fitting to the semester binding of our project work, “The application of natural science in technology and society”, we will try to understand how biology and ecology can be applied to understand how multiple stressors derived from anthropogenic and natural causes (agitated by human activities) cumulatively affect marine environments and, consequently, our society. We will work with multiple stressors which affect marine ecosystems in the Anthropocene. This means that all the stressors we are looking at somehow are human induced. Most of the stressors are related to climate change, for instance: sea level rise, acidification and long term oxygen depletion. But the stressors also include: overfishing, contamination and sedimentation, due to run-off from agriculture, and forestry. These stressors are caused by humans, more precisely, by human technologies. So we can see that all the mentioned stressors in the example are constructed by us ourselves – the society and thus our technologies. However, these stressors do not only affect the marine ecosystem itself but the impacts are falling back on us as well. In this way, multiple stressors affecting the marine ecosystem in the Anthropocene becomes a concern that our society has to be aware of.

Theory

The Anthropocene: Human impact upon the biosphere

 
“Over the past 50 years, humans have changed the world’s ecosystems more rapidly and extensively than in any other comparable period in human history.” \citep{Steffen_2007} But how does that apply to marine ecosystems? Around 70 percent of the earth surface is covered by water, and the ocean represents roughly 99 percent of earths habitats, hence marine ecosystems play a major role, concerning the health status of earth's biosphere. 
It’s argued that the scope of human activity has reached such a pervasive and all-encompassing level that our impacts on the biosphere and the physical characteristics of Earth will mark history on a geological timescale. In this new epoch, the Anthropocene, humans have arguably become the main driver of change in the environment. 
The word ‘Anthropocene’ was first used by the researcher Paul J. Crutzen in 2002 as assigning the term ‘Anthropocene’ to our current, mainly human influenced period following the Holocene. Different authors already used similar words before, such as Antonia Stoppani in 1873 speaking about a new kind of influence on earth comparable to natural influences and V. I. Vernadsky describing the growing impact of humans as an evolutionary process on earth. \citep{Crutzen_2002}
The Anthropocene began with the industrialization and James Watt’s invention of the steam engine in 1784. The humans started to expand their use of fossil fuels and their CO2 emissions. \citep{Steffen_2007,Crutzen_2002} It is suggested, that the current human impact last for long periods. \citep{Steffen_2007}
The researchers \citet{Steffen_2007} are stating that the Anthropocene proceeded in three different stages. Starting with Pre-Anthropocene events, including the first major use of fossil fuels, leading to the first stage of the Anthropocene: The Industrial Era. This stage, mainly described by the industrialization, started around 1800 and ended around 1945 with the second World War. During this era humans' energy sources were massively altered by the expansion of the use of oil, gas and coil in industry. During the period of time from 1800 until 2000, human energy use grew about 40-fold and global economy increased about 50-fold. Related to this, human population registered enormous growth in numbers. Looking at the impact of the Industrial era on the biosphere by comparing the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere, the preindustrial era accounted an atmospheric CO2 concentration about 270-275 ppmv and rose above 300 ppmv until 1950. By the end of the second stage of the Anthropocene, the Great acceleration, around 2015, the atmospheric CO2 concentration has increased above 380 ppmv and is nowadays about 407 ppmv. This extreme rise in numbers is only a small fragment of the ecological change that happened during the industrial era and the Great acceleration, but gives a good insight in the scope of the interference with natural cycles like the carbon cycle. \citep{Steffen_2007}
During the second stage of the Anthropocene, the global human population has grown to over 6 billion, the global economy experienced a great extension, motorization proceeded and urban areas expanded drastically, leading to widespread habitat loss. This stating only a few aspects of this era characterized by expansion, innovation and discovery. \citep{Steffen_2007}
Humans have shaped and affected the marine ecosystem and thereby marine life, rising more and more with human development. For example about 8% of the primary production of the oceans is used by humans, while that fraction grows to more than 25% in certain areas and for the temperate continental shelf systems to more than one third. Furthermore, by 1995 one fifth of known marine fisheries were overexploited or already exhausted and 44% were at a critical threshold. \citep{Vitousek_1997} This shows the great interference of humankind with the marine ecosystem, especially marine life.
Since 2015, we have entered the third stage of the Anthropocene, named 'Stewards of the Earth System'. Given by the name of this era, now-a-days humans are seen as major geological force,  shaping the future of the earth system. This stage is described by growing awareness, due to more research, global information-flow, more stable and democratic governments, integration into policies concerns and social and cultural concerns. \citep{Steffen_2007}
Our current extraction-consumption based economic model has led to rapid expansion of human habitation and resource extraction causing detrimental effects on the extent and integrity of the ecosystems and habitats that enable rich biodiversity and vital ecosystem services. Furthermore, the resources we extract are often returned to the environment in such a composition or context that they become pollutants, which no longer cycle through the systems in an equilibrium or a beneficial way. Going forward, human activity is likely to cause widespread mass-extinctions and biodiversity loss with adverse consequences for future habitability on earth, should we stay on a business as usual course without altering our ways. Referring to the article “The future of biodiversity”, currently the Earth is in its sixth great extinction event, with rates of species loss growing rapidly for both terrestrial and marine ecosystems \citep{Pimm_1995,Ceballos2015}. The atmospheric concentrations of several important greenhouse gases have increased substantially. As a result the Earth is warming rapidly \citep{2014} and more nitrogen is now converted from the atmosphere into reactive forms by fertilizer production and fossil fuel combustion compared to all of the natural processes in terrestrial ecosystems put together. \citep{Galloway_2002}
Most of the human-induced stress on marine ecosystems can be ascribed to climate change. Climate change being one of the major and most discussed catchphrases in our time, shows that the realization that human activities affecting the structure and functioning of Earths biosphere pervasively is slowely rising arwareness in society  \citep{Steffen_2007} .
Human activities have had a significant impact on the earth’s ecosystems; especially in marine ecosystems. For centuries, ocean have been regarded as an inexhaustible source of food and a convenient dumping ground, too vast to be affected by anything we do. But in the last few decades, it has become increasingly clear that the ocean has limits and that in many important parts of our seas the sustainability thresholds have been breached. \citep{Doney_2010}  \citet{riebesell2017} describes this impact by defining the Anthropocene in “Ocean change: a mix of interacting stressors” as an age in which humans impact the environment and Earths’ ecosystems to the same amount or even more than natural influences. Furthermore, he emphasizes marine ecosystems in this era while describing ocean change as an ongoing process in the whole history of earth and that the present ocean change be unique in the modern time. It be the fastest change and perhaps the biggest influence, that a single species ever had and that species be modern humans – us. \citep{riebesell2017}
For this reason there is a need to understand the ongoing processes of ocean change and the changes in marine ecosystems and their health.
 
Defining Marine Ecosystem Health

The Importance of Understanding Marine Ecosystem Health and their Structure

The marine ecosystem is an important biological system that provides services to humanity as a whole and therefore we are a beneficiary of the ocean for what it provides, but research is stressing the urgency for environmental management of this ecosystem due to the rapidness of its declining state. \citep{Costanza1999} 

Facing the Challenges of Defining Marine Ecosystem Health

Suggested is an application of a human health metaphor which can help us to assess the dilemmas in which we will face when understanding ecosystem health; there are three fundamental issues which are proposed: recognizing the absence of objective criteria for assessing health indicators, recognizing when changes are actual or a false alarm, and the quest to find classifying indicators with attributes of being holistic, early warning or diagnostic. \citep{Rapport_1992a}
Above those issues, we face a two-fold challenge of boosting ecosystem behaviour and multiple stressor knowledge and working on the development of an identification system of indicators while we deal with the anthropogenic stressors which impact the integrity of biological ecosystems. \citep{Rapport_1992a}

Proposing the state of a healthy ecosystem

Having mentioned the importance of evaluating the ecosystem health, it is proper to give a definition of what a healthy ecosystem is. The healthy ecosystem is characterized as one that is capable of maintaining its vigor (overall metabolism and energy flow) and organization (diversity of interactions between ecosystem components) over time, in the presence of a stress (resilience). Focusing on the ecosystem resilience, it has two main components, the length of time (RT) that a system needs to recover from stress and the magnitude of stress (Ms), from which the system can return to its former state. \citep{Costanza1999} These factors are important because ecosystems show little change under a stress until they reach a critical threshold, related to RT and Ms. After this point, they react in highly unpredictable (non-linear) ways and this may lead to their collapse. \citep{Scheffer2015}
