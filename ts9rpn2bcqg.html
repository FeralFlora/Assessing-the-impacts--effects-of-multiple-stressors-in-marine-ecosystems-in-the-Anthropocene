1
Problem formulation and the purpose of the assessment
What first needs to be done is to formulate the problem, what includes setting the protection goals, the risk assessment objectives and important ecological considerations.
2
Define baseline
Defining which are the reference points of interest is one of the first steps that scientist should take.Setting clear historical baselines is essential in examining degraded ecosystems to diminish the risk of imprecise assumptions and provide a solid base for future comparisons.
3
Conceptual model of ecosystem (focus on elements and processes)
a)      identify bio-ecological receptors and their relationships
b)     Relate the model to the concept of ecosystem health
After finding the potential receptors, the ecosystem is represented in a conceptual framework focusing on the receptors, their interactions and the ecological processes. This may help to evaluate the ecosystem health, useful later on to know how much is the biological system moved from its operating range.
4
Identify the presence of drivers
Having determined which parts of the examined ecosystem are dysfunctional,the next step is to identify the ecosystem threats and the drivers of its degradation.
5
Ranking stressors based on their individual effects
a)      Stressor hierarchy
Once the drivers in the study area are identified, an inventory of the consequent stressors is done. The broader the list, the more accurate the assessment will be. Afterwards, the different stressors will be classified according to the magnitude of their impacts in order to establish a hierarchy, emphasizing in the key stressors.
 
6
Cumulative impact mapping
Generating a regional map that shows the potential cumulative impacts as a result of the stressorsâ€™ interactions allows for the identification of overlapping effects.
7
Describing the combined effect of the stressors and taking into account their potential interactions
a)      Synergistic, additive, and antagonistic
 
In this step, the aim is to ascertain whether the interactions found in the cumulative impact mapping are synergistic, additive, or antagonistic. Thus, it is possible to evaluate their combined consequences in the ecosystem. 
 
8
Conceptual framework of stressor and ecosystem interactions
From the above steps, the end goal is to synthesize the information into a concise conceptual framework that integrates stressor interactions, ecosystem dynamics and the holistic effects of potential interaction pathways between these.
9
Quantification
1. Statistical or mechanistic modelling
a)      Characterizing driver-response relationships in marine pelagic ecosystems for improved ocean management
b)     Integrated presentation of ecological risk from multiple stressors
c)      A new paradigm of quantifying ecosystem stress through chemical signatures
Having established a conceptual framework that describes the ecosystem to be assessed and the present stressors, characterized in relation to the ecosystem, the next and ultimate challenge is to analyze the system to derive an assessment of what impact the stressors cumulatively impose under observed conditions or predicted scenarios - taking into account non-additive interactions, potential non-linear responses and confounding dynamic processes, environmental state fluxes and life stage (Extending Vulnerability Assessment to Include Life Stages Considerations).
