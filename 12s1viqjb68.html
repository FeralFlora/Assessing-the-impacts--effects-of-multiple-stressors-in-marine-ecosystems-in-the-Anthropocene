4. Present drivers
The coral ecosystem map from the previous step has established a functional ecological understanding to guide the rest of the assessment. From this point on, we can begin to create an overview of the present drivers, defined as underlying causes of change (either positive or negative) in the environment (FIX ME -->) (Outlook Report, 2014). Respectively, a driver is a factor that creates stressors, and the stressors, in turn, are the specific effects (arising from the driver) that cause stress. A single driver may drive several stressors, which may interact. For the purposes of the "Reef 2050 Integrated Monitoring and Reporting Program" the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park Authority propose six broad drivers that cause change and pressure on the Great Barrier Reef:
  1. Climate change
  2. Population growth
  3. Economic growth
  4. Technological developments
  5. Societal attitudes
  6. Governance systems
Whilst informative about the underlying factors that influence human activity levels and the resultant environmental pressures, these categories are quite coarse and don't directly inform the assessment about the present drivers, as defined above. All the drivers found in the previous step lead to a series of stressors. The stressors affecting the Great Barrier Reef are degraded water quality, ocean acidification, increased sea temperature, low salinity, high nutrients, presence of pollutants -e.g. pesticides-, increased population of crown of thorns starfish, shipping incidents -e.g. oil spills-, ballast water -e.i. invasive species-, anchoring, and weather events. \citep{Schoepf2013,Brodie2012,unknown} Once the stressors are identified, their impact is measured to establish a hierarchy pointing the key stressors. 6. Cumulative impact mapping
After having identified and ranked the stressors impacting GBR, we continue by creating a cumulative impact map. This is a very useful procedure that gives us a visualization of the problem and allows us to see the whole picture. As we mentioned in the Theory part the professor  (B. S. Halpern et al., 2008) first intoduced this concept and he provides a good starting point for future assessment attempts on his paper  "A Global Map of Human Impact on Marine Ecosystems". Another example of how the cumulative effects of different stressors can be combined in a map is demonstrated on professor's \citep{Maynard_2015} research paper "Great Barrier Reef No-Take Areas Include a Rangeof Disturbance Regimes " in which four stressors(thermal stress events, damaging waves from cyclones, Crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks and freshwater inundation ) were examined. Although this method helps scientists to identify and visualize high exposure areas, more improvements should be done in order to provide more solid and representative results, as for example it takes into acount only the additive synergies between the stressors and data gaps, often, affect its sufficency.  
7. Describing the combined effect of the stressors and taking into account their potential interactions
+++++ AOP to identify the interactions
The interactions between stressors in the Great Barrier Reef were identified in the step coming before, but it is still not clear whether they are additive, synergistic, or antagonistic. To solve this matter, simulated additive effects between all the interacting stressors are calculated. The method to assess additive effects, which are the sum of the single ones, is found in the following paper: Synergistic impacts of global warming on the resilience of coral reefs \citep{unknown}. Then, the expectation is compared with the reality. If the results match or are similar, then the interaction is additive. On the other hand, if the response is considerably higher, means that the combined stressors act synergistic, and, if it is lower, the interaction is antagonistic. An example that shows the complexity of this assessment is the mutual effects caused by ocean acidification and increased sea surface temperature. While the interaction between these two stressors on the dissolution of carbonate in coral reefs is antagonistic \citep{Trnovsky2016}, what is positive to coral's health, they can also interact synergistically making corals more susceptible to bleaching. \citep{Anthony2008}
