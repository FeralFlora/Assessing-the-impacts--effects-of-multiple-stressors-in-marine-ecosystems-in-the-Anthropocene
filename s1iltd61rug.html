The ecosystem model should be developed with ecosystem health considerations in mind that seek to identify the critical components of the ecosystem such as keystone species, foundation and habitat forming species. Furthermore, it should associate these receptors with appropriate bioindicators, which may prove to be challenging. There is an ongoing need to develop dynamic ecosystem models and validate these with empirical analyses to refine proposed indicators, but integrative developments whereby indices are aggregated and combined according to their complementary strengths show promise - especially when combining indicators related to structural attributes at the community-level with biotic indices based on functional and/or structural attributes of sentinel or indicator species. In relation to the ecosystem health understanding, the combination of indicators should seek to represent different aspects of ecosystem health. \citep{Rombouts2013a,Mart_nez_Crego_2010}.

Challenges

Assessing the impacts and effects of multiple stressors in marine ecosystems itself is one of the biggest challenges in science. \citep{VanDenBrink2016} But why is this assessment that challenging? Trying to make a holistic or ecosystem-based perspective is one of the key challenges in stress ecology and ocean management today and especially as the Anthropocene progresses. This is due to the complexity of the topic and a huge lack of data, such as the still only partly understood interactions among multiple stressors in marine ecosystems. Therefore, considerable future reseacrh is needed to improve the contribution these system models make to understand interactions among multiple stressors.\citep{VanDenBrink2016}
Fundamental questions in this research section are, whether the expected joint effect of a cumulative impact and effect is higher or lower than the intensity of each single stressor. Moreover, it is critical to assess whether the various stressors are overlapping in time and space to result in a potential cumulative effect. \citep{Judd_2015} Therefore, marine stressors can be grouped into stressors acting globally and those that act at in a local to regional level but occur globally. (Porter et al., 1999) Additionally, the recovery potential of an ecosystem might be important to differenciate between multi- and univariate stressors' impacts. Another question is whether the cumulative effect of multivariate stressors just quantitatively differ from the effect of single stressors or if there are new effects and responses, which differ from the expected or predicted outcome. \citep{Judd_2015} This problem is shown by categorizing the different stressors and their interactions into synergistic, antagonistic and additive effects. \citep{Crain2008} These considered interactions and decisions about the necessary input data, the running of the model and the description of the final uncertainty in the model influence the expected size, such as magnitude, extent and intensity of the stressors caused by the given combination of stressors. Another problem in relation to the aforementioned balancing act between accuracy and practicality is the lack of consistency or standardization in approaches, definitions and vocabulary of cumulative impacts assessment. This lack of clarity leads to a recommendation of looking critical on defining the scope of the assessment and the best fitting methodologies. As one can see before, the recommended steps of assessing multiple stressors are a mixture of different methods, collecting the best fitting methodologies. Although there still exist various definitions of how to describe cumulative impact assessment, one can say, that all cumulative effect assessments are based on an ecosystem approach. \citep{Judd_2015} This need for the development of an ecosystem based approach to the assessment of cumulative impacts from multiple stressors has been recognized and emphasized for decades \citep{Wrona1996}. Therefore, it is important to get an integrated knowledge of ecosystem structure and function, a holistic overview of the interacting stressors at different organizational, spatial and temporal scales within the ecosystem and to develop management strategies that are ecological, anticipatory and ethical. \citep{Christie_1986,Judd_2015,Wrona1996} 
So cumulative effects assessments involve assessing and evaluating the interactions between multiple stressors with multiple effects on multiple ecosystem components. This complex web of interactions of direct and indirect effects of multiple stressors quickly generates a complicated and unwieldy network of multiple interactions. How to manage and assess this complexity is a challenge to all forms of cumulative effects assessment. The assessment of multiple stressors and their impacts and effects is clearly impractical. Therefore the application of environmental risk assessment principles allows a meaningful and achievable assessment for multiple stressors. \citep{Judd_2015} Environmental risk assessment is a process of choice, based on identifying the likely consequences of different oeffects and impacts and selecting the best actions  to minimising and managing environmental risks. \citep{University2011}

By applying our proposed steps explained in the results, we'll make and explore a hypothetical assessment of the Great Barrier Reef (GBR), using articles previously analyzed during our research process to give practical examples, whenever possible, on how each step can be executed and its goal achieved.  

1. Problem formulation

The Great Barrier Reef is the largest living structure on Earth, stretching for over 2300 km in the north-eastern region of Australia. While being mostly known for the endless network of coral reefs, its intricate architecture also provides a home for a great amount of animals and plants, creating one of the most biodiverse and complex ecosystem on the planet. Unfortunately, the functional biodiversity of the Great Barrier Reef renders its system highly susceptible to even the smallest change in the balance acquired with thousands of years of evolution \cite{Mouillot_2014}.
