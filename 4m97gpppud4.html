Criteria for judging multiple stressor methods
(Maybe a better introduction for this section) In this part, we are going to present the criteria that we think a method for assessing the effects of multiple stressors should meet. In a next level, using these criteria we will try to create a ranking table for the different categories we found in the section above.  This will be a helpful tool for the scientists to choose the appropriate assessment method for each examined environment. These criteria can be concluded as:
  • Measurability describes the ability of a method to use indicators which are easy and cost-effective to measure, collect and calculate.
  • Sensitivity refers to the ability of a method to provide early warning of a change and identify potential interactions between the multiple stressors over time. 
  • Scalability introduces the importance of a method to be applied to a different spatial scale and still retain its ability to indicate the change of interest and provide sufficient results for the whole ecosystem.
  • Transferability is the scope that a method should be useful and easily applied to other locations and hence studies, giving reliable and quantified information about all parts of the world.
  •  Communicability describes whether or not the results of an assessment method can be easily communicated to a wide audience. As multiple stressor issues usually affect the communities, information and understanding of the problem from the majority of the community is a crucial step for further actions.
Except for these criteria, we think that scientists should, also, take into account the different dynamic processes that occur in an environment and consider about the shifting baseline syndrome. More specifically, in some cases, dynamic processes such as oceanographic currents or immigration are fundamentally important and affect the overall results, as it may change how a stressor is distributed or spread its impacts among different locations \cite{Halpern_2009}. As regard the shifting baseline syndrome, it is yet unclear what baselines should the different methods use as a starting point. Relying exclusively on current data, ignoring the historical references, may limit our understanding of the ecosystem fuctioning and increase the possibilty of a sudden  collapse of the ecosystem services\cite{Dearing_2012}.
Furthermore, we believe that a sufficient assessment method should be capable of recognizing whether the interactions between the multiple stressors are synergistic, additive or antagonistic.
