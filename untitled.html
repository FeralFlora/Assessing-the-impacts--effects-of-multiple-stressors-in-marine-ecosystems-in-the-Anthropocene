The Anthropocene: Human impact upon the biosphere

 
“Over the past 50 years, humans have changed the world’s ecosystems more rapidly and extensively than in any other comparable period in human history.” \citep{Steffen_2007} But how does that apply on marine ecosystems? Around 70 percent of the earth surface is covered by water, and the ocean represents 99 percent of earths habitats, this leads to the assumption, that marine ecosystem play a major role, concerning the health status of earths ecosystem. 
It’s argued that the scope of human activity has reached such a pervasive and all-encompassing level that our impacts on the biosphere and the physical characteristics of Earth will mark history on a geological timescale. In this new epoch, the Anthropocene, humans have arguably become the main driver of change in the environment. 
The word ‘Anthropocene’ was first used by the researcher Paul J. Crutzen in 2002 as assigning the term ‘Anthropocene’ to our current, mainly human influenced period following the Holocene. Different authors already used similar words before, such as Antonia Stoppani in 1873 speaking about a new kind of influence on earth comparable to natural influences and V. I. Vernadsky describing the growing impact of humans as an evolutionary process on earth. \citep{Crutzen_2002}
 The Anthropocene began with the industrialization and James Watt’s invention of the steam engine in 1784. The humans started to expand their use of fossil oils and their CO2 emissions. \citep{Steffen_2007,Crutzen_2002} It is suggested, that the current human impact last for long periods. \citep{Steffen_2007}
The researchers \citet{Steffen_2007} are stating that the Anthropocene proceeded in three different stages. Starting with Pre-Anthropocene events, including the first major use of fossil fuels, leading to the first stage of the Anthropocene: The Industrial Era. This stage, mainly described by the industrialization, started around 1800 and ended around 1945 with the second World War. During this era humans energy source was massively altered by the expansion of the use of oil, gas and coil in industry. During the period of time from 1800 until 2000, humans energy used grew about 40-fold and global economy increased about 50-fold. Related to this, human population registered enormous growth in numbers. Looking at the impact of the Industrial era on the biosphere by comparing the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere, the preindustrial era accounted an atmospheric CO2 concentration about 270-275 ppmv and rose above 300 ppmv until 1950. By the end of the second stage of the Anthropocene, the Great acceleration, around 2015, the atmospheric CO2 concentration has increased above 380 ppmv. This extreme rise in numbers is only a small fragment of the ecological change that happened during the industrial era and the Great acceleration, but gives a good insight in the scope of the interference. \citep{Steffen_2007}
During the second stage of the Anthropocene, humans population has grown over 6 billion, the global economy experienced a great extension, motorization proceeded and urban areas expanded drastically. This stating only a few aspects of this era characterized by expansion, innovation and discovery. \citep{Steffen_2007}
Humans have shaped and effected the marine ecosystem, rising more and more with human development. For example about 8% of the primary production of the oceans is used by humans, while that fraction grows for areas to more than 25% and for temperate continental shelf systems to more than one third. Furthermore, by 1995 one fifth of known marine fisheries were overexploided or already exhausted and 44% were at a critical threshold. \citep{Vitousek_1997} This shows the great interference of humankind on the marine ecosystem, especially marine life.
Since 2015, we have entered the third stage of the Anthropocene, named 'Stewards of the Earth System'. Given by the name of this era, now-a-days humans are seen as major geological force,  shaping the future of the earth system. This stage is described by growing awareness, due to more research, global information-flow, more stable and democratic governments, integration into policies concerns and social and cultural concerns. \citep{Steffen_2007}
Our current extraction-consumption based economic model has led to rapid expansion of human habitation and resource extraction causing detrimental effects on the extent and integrity of the ecosystems and habitats that enable rich biodiversity and vital ecosystem services. Furthermore, the resources we extract are often returned to the environment in such a composition or context that they become pollutants, which no longer cycle through the systems in an equilibrium or a beneficial way. Going forward, human activity is likely to cause widespread mass-extinctions and biodiversity loss with adverse consequences for future habitability on earth, should we stay on a business as usual course without altering our ways. Referring to the article “The future of biodiversity”, currently the Earth is in its sixth great extinction event, with rates of species loss growing rapidly for both terrestrial and marine ecosystems \citep{Pimm_1995}. The atmospheric concentrations of several important greenhouse gases have increased substantially. As a result the Earth is warming rapidly \citep{2014} and more nitrogen is now converted from the atmosphere into reactive forms by fertilizer production and fossil fuel combustion compared to all of the natural processes in terrestrial ecosystems put together. \citep{Galloway_2002}
Most of the human-induced stress on marine ecosystems can be ascribed to climate change. Climate change being one of the major and most discussed catchphrases in our time, shows that “(..) the recognition that human activities are indeed affecting the structure and functioning of the Earth System (..) is filtering through to decision-making at many levels”. \citep{Steffen_2007}
Human activities have had a significant impact on the earth’s ecosystems; especially in marine ecosystems. For centuries, ocean have been regarded as an inexhaustible source of food and a convenient dumping ground, too vast to be affected by anything we do. But in the last few decades, it has become increasingly clear that the ocean has limits and that in many important parts of our seas the sustainability thresholds have been breached. \citep{Doney_2010}  \citet{riebesell2017} describes this impact by defining the Anthropocene in “Ocean change: a mix of interacting stressors” as an age in which humans impact the environment and Earths’ ecosystems to the same amount or even more than natural influences. Furthermore, he emphasizes marine ecosystems in this era while describing ocean change as an ongoing process in the whole history of earth and that the present ocean change be unique in the modern time. It be the fastest change and perhaps the biggest influence, that a single species ever had and that species be modern humans – us. \citep{riebesell2017}
For this reason there is a need to understand the ongoing processes of ocean change and the changes in marine ecosystems and their health.
