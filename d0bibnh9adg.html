Multiple stressors and assessment methods

As the preceding sections have shown, the challenges of effectively and accurately assessing and predicting the outcomes of multiple stressors acting in parallel on marine ecosystems lie in the inherent complexity of the task - arising both from the interwoven nature of ecosystems with webbed relationships of direct and  indirect interdependence, as well as the overlapping effects of the stressors themselves. The ecosystems affected by multiple stressors can not be assumed to be in any one stable state, when assessed long-term or even inter-annually, as the ongoing effects of human impacts and systemic alterations to the biosphere continue to develop in magnitude, multitude and spatial extent, thus forcing future assessment and management efforts to take on a more holistic, dynamic and earth-system / ecologically insightful approach to avoid non-linear effects and resultant ecological surprises, which might otherwise thwart the efforts to stave of the ongoing \citep{Ceballos_2015} (or conservatively, impending \citep{Rothman_2017}) Sixth Mass Extinction. As a result, given the multivariate nature and complexity of the issue, univariate impact assessments may well be rendered effectively obsolete in most future oceanic contexts, except when conducted as part of systemic analyses.
Paradoxically, there is simultaneously a need to pay greater attention to specific characteristics of stressors, biological receptors and their physical environments and the dynamics and interactions between these, and a need to simplify these complexities in a way that allows for effective analysis in an timely manner, balancing holism and information overload. The latter need is underlined by the urgency of the biodiversity and ecosystem stability crisis.
We'll now consider the conventional approaches to impact assessments and highlight some of their strengths as well as the pitfalls that have inspired the efforts to develop new approaches, both in extension of the existing methods and in novel ways. Thereafter, we'll give a rough outline of general themes in multiple stressor assessments, which are expanded upon in the results and discussion section of the report.

Conventional ecological risk and impact assessments and their pitfalls 

Ecotoxicology, "the branch of Toxicology concerned with the study of toxic effects, caused by natural or synthetic pollutants, to the constituents of ecosystems, animal (including human), vegetable and microbial, in an integral context.” \citep{Truhaut1977}, developed from toxicology's initial focus on the dose-response of individuals within test populations to chemicals into a similar measure for ecological toxicity, where a concentration of a chemical in an environment amounts to a certain degree of toxicity concluding with an ecological risk assessment. Viewed as an attempt at an integration of ecology and toxicology,  ecotoxicology has been criticised for being too simplistic - leaning more towards toxicology than ecology and failing to account sufficiently for the categorical difference between an individual and communities, where population size and constitution are deciding factors, rather than the response mode of an individual.
Although research in ecotoxicology has progressed substantially from simplified, single-species laboratory tests, the ecotoxicological data that provide the scientific input to risk assessments are based primarily on such simple tests.
To extrapolate from these simplified single-species laboratory tests to complex ecosystems, two conceptual approaches are generally applied:
  1. PNECs (Predicted No Effect Concentrations)
  2. SSDs (Species Sensitivity Distributions)
PNECs are thresholds that assign the upper concentration limit until which ecological effects remaining unlikely. SSDs are applications of available critical response data such as NOEC (No-Observed-Effect Concentrations) or EC50 (sublethal response in 50% of the population) information, from either prospective or retrospective risk assessments, as a function of chemical concentrations to derive a frequency distribution of affected species. When SSDs are applied, they are used to inform about a concentration that will leave a large fraction (90–95%) of the affected species above the critical-effect threshold, which is interpreted as allowing the continued integrity of intact ecosystems. \citep{Calow2003}. As "intact ecosystems" may be in short order today, this assumption may be increasingly unrealistic / optimistic.
As noted by \citet{Segner2014} "This approach neglects the fact that toxic chemicals in the environment do not act in isolation, but their impact on biota is modulated by the presence of other biotic and abiotic stressors." Therein lies the major pitfall of the current ecotoxilogical approaches to risk and impact assessments, and the crux of the multiple stressor challenge.
The challenge is aggravated by changing  environmental conditions and their increasing variability, brought about by widespread human impacts, as explored in section \ref{384501}, with overlapping stressors in most if not all cases \citep{Gutt2015a}. In specific cases, stressors in localized environments have numbered as high as 23 stressors, with an average of 13 stressors in each location, specifically in this case along the California Current in the Pacific Ocean \citep{Halpern_2009}. This change in environmental conditions, referring to both biochemical and physical characteristics of the marine environment, alters the operation of pollutants with effects on transport, transformation and distribution of these, whilst also altering behavior of native and potential invasive species to disrupt ecosystem composition, species distributions and their habitats \citep{Couillard2008}. Furthermore, this change in environmental conditions can affect the vulnerability of aquatic species to toxic chemicals (or xenobiotics, for a more encompassing term)  and in turn these may affect aquatic species' ability to adapt to changing environmental conditions and stress \citep{Couillard2008a,Hooper2013}, leading to more unpredictable results and otherwise unexpected outcomes and ecological surprises \citep{Paine1998,Christensen2006}.
